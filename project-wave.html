WAVE — A Tangible
Interaction Experiment

A mix of art and technology. WAVE lets anyone create and control sound with a simple wave of their hand — no buttons, no prior knowledge, just gesture and feedback.

About

Sound You Can Touch

WAVE is a tangible interaction experiment that explores how simple horizontal hand movements can be used to create and control sound — engaging and captivating the user through audio and visual feedback.

The project sits at the intersection of physical computing, generative audio, and expressive design. It was built to challenge the assumption that making music requires screens, buttons, or musical training.

Design Question

The Thought

"Can simple spatial gestures enable anyone to feel a sense of musical control — without relying on screen interfaces, buttons, or prior musical knowledge?"

Goal

What We Were Designing For

To design an expressive interaction where the experience itself communicates how it works — no instructions needed.

  • Users understand cause-and-effect within seconds
  • Musical output feels immediately responsive
  • Exploration feels playful, not intimidating
  • Feedback is multi-modal — sound + light + visuals
  • Low learning curve regardless of musical background
Interaction Model

How It Works

The interaction is deliberately minimal — one gesture, multi-modal feedback. The system encourages users to experiment, observe, and refine movement, gradually building a mental model of how their actions affect the output.

The gesture loop:

  • The user moves their hands parallel to two ultrasonic sensors
  • Distance from the sensor controls pitch and sound parameters
  • LED lights provide immediate spatial feedback
  • Audio output drives real-time generative visuals in TouchDesigner

Key design decisions:

  • No buttons or menus — pure gesture
  • One primary input: hand movement in space
  • Feedback is multi-modal: audio + visual + light
  • System rewards curiosity over precision
User Journey

Four Stages of Engagement

Every first-time user moved through a consistent arc — from cautious curiosity to confident exploration. The design was built to support each stage.

01
Discovery
Users approach the device with a simple instruction: move your hand horizontally.
02
Recognition
Users notice a direct relationship between hand movement and sound variation.
03
Adaptation
Users adjust their movements based on feedback, developing a personal interaction style.
04
Exploration
Users experiment with speed and range to push the boundaries of the system.
Prototype

Building It

The prototype was built using off-the-shelf hardware and open-source software — the goal was speed of iteration, not polish.

  • Ultrasonic sensors for gesture detection
  • Arduino for processing input
  • TouchDesigner for sound generation and audio-reactive visuals
  • LEDs for immediate spatial feedback
User Observations & Learnings

What Testing Revealed

Through informal testing and observation, clear patterns emerged around what worked and where users struggled.

What Worked

  • Users quickly understood that movement affects sound
  • First-time users were willing to experiment without prompting
  • Children treated it as play; adults treated it as performance

Where Users Struggled

  • Precise control was difficult due to sensor noise
  • Sudden note jumps broke the feeling of intention

Key insight: Users consistently described WAVE as "feeling like conducting an orchestra" — the spatial, full-body nature of interaction made it far more expressive than expected from a prototype.

Reflection

What WAVE Taught Me

WAVE helped me understand that tangible interaction design is less about novelty and more about trust between the user and the system. In a world where people are so used to digital interfaces, a tangible object invites curiosity — the physical presence reduces fear of "doing it wrong."

Even small inconsistencies in feedback can significantly impact how confident users feel while interacting. This project sharpened my focus on designing interactions that communicate clearly through the medium itself — not through instructions.
